Historical Terminology Explained

Sugar-plums

"...While visions of **sugar-plums** danced in their heads;"

A type of hard sugar candy or **comfit** popular from the 17th to 19th centuries. They were typically made by coating a seed (like caraway or anise) or a nut (like an almond) with many layers of hardened sugar syrup. They were luxury treats for special occasions, and often did not contain any actual plums.

'Kerchief

"And mamma in her **'kerchief**, and I in my cap,"

Short for **head-kerchief** (a scarf or covering worn on the head, often tied under the chin). In the 19th century, women wore kerchiefs and men wore caps to bed to stay warm in cold, drafty houses, as a form of insulation and also to keep hair clean and tidy.

Peddler

"And he looked like a **peddler**, just opening his pack."

A **traveling salesman** or *itinerant vendor*. This person carried a large pack of goods (notions, tools, or small toys) on their back to sell in remote areas. The narrator compares St. Nicholas, with his bundle of toys, to this common historical figure.

Coursers

"More rapid than eagles his **coursers** they came,"

A poetic or older term for **swift horses** or, in this context, the **reindeer** that pull the sleigh. The term emphasizes their speed, power, and spirited nature.

Ere

"But I heard him exclaim, **ere** he drove out of sight,"

An archaic word that simply means **"before."** St. Nicholas gave his final exclamation ("Happy Christmas to all...") *before* he disappeared. |

Lustre

"Gave the **lustre** of mid-day to objects below."

A poetic word for **shine**, **glow**, or **brilliance**. The light of the moon reflecting on the fresh snow makes the night world as bright as the middle of the day.
